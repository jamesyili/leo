# What to Say When You Don't Know What to Say

**Source:** https://jeffersonfisher.substack.com/p/what-to-say-when-you-dont-know-what
**Ingested:** 2026-04-02
**Tags:** communication, soft-skills

---

There's a moment most people know well.

Someone shares something heavy — a loss, a diagnosis, a heartbreak, a failure. Or maybe the conversation takes a sudden turn you weren't ready for. Someone asks you something you weren't expecting. Silence fills the space.

And your mind goes completely blank.

You want to say the right thing. But nothing comes. So you either say something you immediately regret, or you say nothing at all and walk away feeling like you let that person down.

I've been there. More times than I'd like to admit. And what I've learned — both in the courtroom and in life — is that not knowing what to say doesn't make you a bad communicator. It makes you human.

The good news? There are a handful of phrases and principles that work in almost every situation. You don't need perfect words. You just need the right ones.

Why We Freeze in the First Place

When we don't know what to say, it's usually for one of three reasons:

We're ***afraid of saying the wrong thing*** and making it worse. We care about the person and the pressure of that care shuts us down.

We're ***waiting for the perfect response*** — something profound, something that fixes it. But perfect doesn't exist in real conversation, and waiting for it leaves the other person standing alone in their moment.

Or we're ***so focused on ourselves*** — on how we're coming across, on what they must think of us — that we forget the conversation was never really about us to begin with.

Once you understand why you freeze, you can interrupt the pattern. And the way you interrupt it is simpler than you think.

The Most Powerful Thing You Can Do: Just Be Present

Before we get to specific phrases, here's the most important thing I can tell you:

***Most people don't need you to fix anything. They need to feel less alone.***

When someone is going through something hard, the instinct is to reach for a solution — to say something helpful, something wise. But more often than not, what they actually need is just for someone to sit with them in it. To acknowledge what they're carrying without rushing past it.

Presence is not passive. It is one of the most active, courageous things one person can offer another. And it starts before you even open your mouth.

What to Say: Practical Phrases That Actually Work

Here are phrases I come back to again and again — in difficult conversations, in moments of grief, in those unexpected turns that leave us speechless.

***When someone shares hard news:***

"*I'm so sorry. I don't have the right words, but I want you to know I'm here.*"

This is one of the most honest and comforting things you can say. It doesn't pretend to have answers. It doesn't minimize what the person is going through. It simply says: I see you, and I'm not going anywhere.

***When you genuinely don't know how to respond:***

"*That means a lot that you told me that. Give me a moment.*"

Pausing is not weakness. It's respect. It says this conversation matters enough for you to think before you speak. Most people will appreciate that more than a rushed, hollow response.

***When someone is visibly upset and you don't know why:***

"*I can see something's going on. I'm here if you want to talk and I'm also here if you don't.*"

This removes the pressure. It opens the door without forcing anyone through it. It honors both the person's pain and their right to process it on their own timeline.

***When you said the wrong thing and realize it:***

"*I don't think that came out the way I meant it. Can I try again?*"

This is one of the most disarming phrases in communication. It takes courage to say. And it almost always softens the moment immediately.

***When someone asks you a question you're not ready for:***

"*That's a great question. I want to give you a real answer let me think about that*."

You are never obligated to fill silence with noise. Saying you need a moment is not a sign of weakness. It's a sign of someone who takes the conversation seriously.

What NOT to Say

As important as knowing what to say is knowing what to avoid. A few phrases that tend to make things worse, even when they're meant well:

"***Everything happens for a reason***." This rarely lands the way we hope. It can feel dismissive of someone's pain, even if that's not the intent.

"***I know exactly how you feel***." You don't. No one does. Even if you've been through something similar, their experience is their own. A better alternative: "I can only imagine how hard this must be."

"***At least..***." Any sentence that starts with "at least" tends to minimize. "At least you still have your health." "At least it wasn't worse." These minimize before they comfort.

When in doubt, say less. A quiet, steady presence will always outperform a nervous string of words that were never really meant to help the other person just to ease your own discomfort.

**The Principle Behind All o**f It

Every phrase I've shared today comes back to one idea:

***Good communication is not about impressing people with your words. It's about making them feel heard.***

You don't have to be eloquent. You don't have to have the perfect thing ready. You just have to be willing to show up, stay present, and let the other person know they matter to you.

That's it. That's the whole thing.

The next time you're in a moment where the words won't come — take a breath. Remember it's not about you. And say something small and honest.

Small and honest will always beat clever and empty.
