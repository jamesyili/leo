Performance reviews are your annual chance to get actual feedback on how you are doing and to get performance-based raises. 

You have *more* influence on the outcome of your review than you may think.  

If you want to get the most out of your performance review, you must treat it like something you own.

I have spent 27+ years in my career (12 in startups and 15 at Amazon) managing at different times up to 800 employees. I have received performance reviews, given hundreds of them, seen them go well, and seen them go very badly.

In this newsletter, we breakdown 11 steps (plus your timeline summary with key actions) to get the most out of yours.

1. Start With Clarity

In a performance review, your main goal is to get clarity. 

Of course, you want to get recognition, praise, and a raise, but these are of secondary value to clarity that tells you how to keep succeeding and moving up.

**You want to know what you are doing well so that you can do more of it, and you want to know what to fix and improve at.**

Clarity is the goal. 

Not validation. 

Not comfort. 

Not winning an argument.

If you walk out of a performance review without being able to articulate what you are doing well, what you need to improve, and what your manager expects next, you did not get the most out of it.

2. Do Not Wait for the Annual Review

Formal feedback sessions ramp up the pressure and expectation. 

Almost every advice on this topic says that informal and more frequent review sessions are better for understanding how you are doing than the single official review.

When feedback is rare, your 12-month performance review turns out to be what your manager remembers from your last couple of months (recency bias). 

What you did nine months ago may be largely forgotten.

If you want to get the most out of your performance review, you cannot let it be the first time you hear important feedback. 

**You must regularly ask for feedback in your 1:1’s, after big meetings, and after launches. **

**You also ask for it anytime something feels off.**

The faster you get feedback, the more specific it can be and the better you can adapt.

When feedback is close to the event, I (your manager) can tell you, *“Yesterday in our meeting you interrupted Amanda several times. I saw Amanda appeared to be upset.”*

That is specific, actionable, and useful.

When feedback comes six months later, it becomes vague. 

Vague feedback is hard to act on.

**If you want a great annual review, earn it through a year of small, frequent feedback loops.**

If you are continuously improving on little things, your overall review will be positive.

3. Own the Context When Managers Change

In big companies, reorganizations between formal performance reviews are common.

I have seen employees have six different managers in one year. 

**Unfortunately, on average, having multiple managers within a year will lower your performance review.**

This is because the new manager will not know you well, and they lose the context that your old manager had. 

They are not sure what you have done and not done. 

This means that if you have had multiple managers, you have to do more work to get a positive review.

**If your old manager is still around, try and get feedback from them and input into the review process. If you know your manager is leaving before your review, try to get them to write something down before they leave.**

**If your company uses 360-degree feedback, make sure peers who know your work are contributing to the review.**

**Finally, talk directly to your current manager. **

Tell them: *“You have only known me a couple months. I need you to do a little extra diligence on my behalf. Here are some good people to talk to.”*

This is not complaining. 

This is managing your career. 

You have the primary responsibility to make sure your performance review reflects what you have done. 

Do not wait and “see how it goes.”

4. Align Performance With Motivation

Performance and motivation are usually tied together. 

Highly motivated people perform better than those who are unmotivated. 

If you are doing work you do not like or that does not motivate you, you are relying on your professionalism to perform well. 

You can only do that for so long before burning out.

**If you want a better performance review, part of getting that is communicating what motivates you and transitioning to that type of work early in the year.** 

The more time you spend doing work you enjoy, the more likely you are to perform well.

You will not be rated highly if you are consistently disengaged, so getting assigned engaging work is helpful for performing well and getting a strong review.
