What Makes a Senior Manager Stand Out

Watch on [YouTube.](https://youtu.be/9edvP1wU9qs?si=hvLtzmntSjiaOe4I)

In this talk, Ethan and [David Anderson](https://www.linkedin.com/in/scarletink/) (Author of [Scarlet Ink](https://www.scarletink.com/)) share the real signals of executive readiness and the patterns that separated reliable Senior Managers from the ones who actually broke through.

This is lived experienced (not theory): Dave as a Director and Ethan as a VP at Amazon, where they evaluated and made promotion decisions. 

**Key Takeaways**

**Senior leaders have an imbalance where 1-2 projects are almost all the value for your org that year — thus, spend 95% of your mental energy on that priority (e.g. communicating it better) to drive it over the line.** Visibility at the higher levels is critical and your priority project is likely the only thing your SVP or VP knows about you and your org. If you got everything else on your plate done, but that one project failed, then you probably failed for the year. And vice versa, if you only got that one project done, and everything else slipped and failed, you probably had a pretty good year. When a peer or stakeholder asks you about how that project is going, you must know today’s status and metrics (not last week’s). 

**Senior leaders in bigger companies oftentimes need to align 5+ organizations to get things done, and as silly as it may sound, strong personal connections is what blast past red tape.** Relationships work because it is a trust filter. When there are competing priorities from 20-40 people, what happens is people learn how to filter most of it out, where you ignore the requests unless you are ordered to do it or it comes from someone you trust. You build strong connections by helping people, explaining the business and customer impact clearly, and not bothering them with small things. 

**If you are not assigned that “big priority” project that will eventually be your promotion doc “headline win”, then look at these 3 options — invent a new solution, make a controversial decision (and be right), or talk to your manager. **At senior levels, volume of projects does not persuade. You need the 1-2 stories that can be told in an elevator pitch that proves you operated at the next level. Otherwise you risk the “lifetime achievement award” promotion but that is the slow and painful path you do not want to take (e.g. *“Wow, Zayn’s been a Senior Manager here since before any of us can remember…he doesn’t suck…so I guess we probably owe it to him to move up”*).

**Invent a new solution —** Dave created a new internal transfer process at Amazon. The previous transfer process was employees could only leave their team if they had been there at least a year. Dave wrote a proposal recommending to not put time limits on transfers. The proposal made it to Jeff Bezos, he approved, and quoted Dave in the announcement. Dave did this as a hobby (not assigned work) and it influenced the entire company (e.g. changed the culture and policies of X teams, only a Director has that level of influence). It became another proof point and clear explanation of doing work at the next level.

**Make a controversial decision (and be right) —** Ethan was leading a new project at Amazon that was split (some wanted to do it some did not). The SVP said *“I probably wouldn’t do this project, but I will leave it to the team.”* Ethan replied *“Well, I would, and unless you order me not to do it, I will do it.”* The project worked out and had a strong business outcome. The SVP remembered that Ethan made a different call than him and it worked out — that stood out and it was cited in Ethan’s promotion doc.

**Talk to your manager —** After you’ve delivered a win (big or small), go to your manager and say *“I need a big win or two (e.g. a new feature I can launch, a new service I can build, lead the new marketing campaign, own the relationship with a marquee customer, sign a big enterprise contract) for my promotional poster…let’s puzzle this out together.” *

**If you’re a Senior Manager (or Senior IC) aiming to break through to the executive level, you need to be prepared for tough situations like these:**

You just had a high-visibility disaster and can see your angry skip coming down the hall. Are you confident in your playbook to stabilize the situation, regain trust, and get moving again? 

Many companies punish failure. Do you understand why you must take calculated risks and how to do so successfully?

*“You’re not strategic enough”* is vague (and annoying) feedback. Can you confidently frame your work to your org and company’s strategy?

Your VP expects leaders to invent features that drive big dollars. Can you do so repeatedly?

Promotions aren’t earned, they’re planned. Are you clear on the agenda in the decision room between your manager and their peers?
