Why do so many performance reviews feel like an ambush?

**In this article, Robert explains the real reason and gives you the four rules every manager must follow to give tough feedback that actually helps:**

Give Feedback Early and Often

Be Direct

Criticize Behavior, Not Character

Make It Action-Oriented

Remember: As a leader, the #1 goal of feedback is to help your team get better.

Imagine you’re sitting in your first annual performance review in your new role.

You expect it to go well, as you’ve mostly heard positive things from your manager throughout the year.

Which is exactly why **you feel blindsided **as your manager shares a litany of critiques of your work and approach. 

They say you’re making rookie mistakes a year into the role. They bring up client emails you sent throughout the year and point out what you did wrong. They raise examples of mistakes from months ago, instances that you can barely remember, and explain that they are unacceptable.

Your pulse quickens as you anticipate what might come next:

***“I’m sorry, but we need to loop HR in to discuss a Performance Improvement Plan. It’s not too late to turn things around, but we are going to have to see quick, material improvement.”***

The meeting ends and you settle back into your desk chair. 

Your brain flies through the stages of grief, starting with shock and denial.

***“Did that really just happen?”***

Then, you start to get angry: 

***“If my manager had such a problem with me, why am I only hearing it now? Why didn’t they correct or address my behavior in the moment if it was so bad? It feels like it’s already too late to fix things.”***

This is obviously unfair. 

And yet, a version of this meeting happens every day, in companies around the world.

It’s especially common in cases where the manager is new and has not learned to have these hard conversations.

**In nearly every case, this type of disconnect is the fault of the leader. **

When a manager delivers negative feedback months after the incidents in question or during infrequent performance reviews, they are failing the people they lead.

Why This Happens

There’s an obvious reason managers make this mistake: **giving difficult feedback is uncomfortable. **

No one enjoys telling people that they’ve done something wrong or that their performance is poor; I know leaders with decades of experience who still agonize over it. The natural impulse is to delay the conversation and hope that the person will figure things out on their own.

Having difficult feedback conversations is one of the most important responsibilities of any manager, whether they lead a team of two or two thousand. And yet, you won’t find this topic in many management training curricula, even at established companies.

Over 15 years of leading a team and training new leaders, I’ve found that no one is naturally excellent at having difficult conversations. **The only way to do this well is to *****practice*****, which is why we created a difficult conversations training program for new managers at my company.**

In these sessions, we give two volunteers a conversation prompt to roleplay for the audience. Each volunteer receives only half the story, which sets up the scenario of a 30-day check-in for a new employee. One plays a manager who needs to warn an employee that they will be let go if they don’t improve, while the other plays an employee who is oblivious about their struggles and is hoping for a promotion.

A group of managers watches the conversation, knowing both sides of the story.

Once the volunteers start roleplaying, the importance of practice becomes tangible to everyone watching. Even in a risk-free, simulated environment, the manager struggles to break the bad news to their employee. They start off with a compliment or some banter, slip in some gentle critiques, then end on a positive note by telling the employee that they believe they have a promising future.

At this point, I say “freeze.” 

Then, I ask the audience, *“how many of you think it is clear to the employee that their job is in jeopardy?”*

Over years of doing this exercise, **I’ve never seen a hand go up.**

That’s where the learning begins. 

Before the volunteers roleplay the conversation a second time—and before the rest of the group pairs up to practice as well—they get feedback from the people watching. Then, the facilitator guides everyone through some pointers on how to give feedback.

The guidance is built around four essential rules managers should follow when giving difficult feedback, which you should take note of for use in your next feedback conversation.

The Four Rules for Giving Difficult Feedback

Rule #1: Give Feedback Early and Often

Your goal should always be to avoid storing up feedback and dropping it on your employee all at once. If you’ve ever had a fight with your partner where you throw a mistake they made months ago in their face, you know how badly this can go.

**Feedback should be given right after the related event so the employee can quickly internalize the lesson and improve next time. **If a person makes a mistake in a client meeting, pull them aside after the fact, explain the mistake, and coach them on how to do better next time. If a person writes a report full of errors, send them redlined edits and comments immediately after so they know what to do differently.

**It’s best to follow the 72-Hour Rule:** either make the effort to deliver the feedback within three days of the incident or let it go. This rule not only helps the person learn, but it keeps you from storing the critique for later, when it does more harm than good.

Rule #2: Be Direct

Many people **mistakenly** soften feedback by sliding it into the middle of an otherwise positive conversation. **This technique is called the Compliment Sandwich.**

They start by telling the person something they do well to make them comfortable. Then, they share the critique or concern. Finally, they close the conversation with another point of praise, to keep the employee from feeling too discouraged.

In practice, this might sound like this:

*Ryan, I really enjoy working with you and especially appreciate how fast and responsive you are. But I think that does sometimes lead you to rush, which leads to your work being sloppy, with some typos and inaccuracies, which you should work on. *

*But overall, you’re doing a great job.*

The manager thinks they’ve highlighted an important issue, but Ryan hears that he’s mostly doing great; **he’s missed the entire point of the conversation.** Often, this means Ryan won’t see this issue as a vital area of improvement or understand why it is so important. 

Because of this disconnect, it’s likely the two parties will be having the same conversation months later.

**Don’t dance around the topic. **

Enter the conversation with only one goal: **to make certain that the employee understands what they need to improve. **

For example, Ryan’s manager might say:

*Ryan, I have to give you some important feedback about your work. While I appreciate how fast you are, that speed seems to lead to a large number of typos and inaccuracies in your work. These mistakes hurt credibility with our clients, and people will lose trust in your work. *

*Instead of rushing to get things out as soon as possible, I recommend investing extra time carefully reviewing your work, correcting any errors, and double-checking any data or stats you use. You’re very talented, but fixing this is vital to ensuring you’re successful here. *

*Do you understand why this is important?*

The manager is not being cruel, just direct. 

They are making it crystal clear that Ryan needs to be more careful in his work, or else his success at the company will be impacted. They are reassuring Ryan a bit by referencing some positive feedback, but that is a minor part of the broader message.

**The best way to be more direct is to make it clear right off the bat that you need to give the person feedback. **

You can say things like:

Just so you know, this is going to be a difficult conversation.

I need to share some really important feedback with you on something that needs to improve in your work.

We have to talk about some issues I’ve seen in your performance lately.

It is possible to challenge someone directly without being a jerk. 

In fact, being direct is the more compassionate thing to do. 

Shielding someone from hard feedback may be comfortable in the moment, but it leads to a much harder conversation down the road: the one where the manager has to let the employee go because they aren’t improving. This concept is the foundation of Kim Scott’s outstanding book, *Radical Candor*, which is a must-read for any new or experienced manager.
