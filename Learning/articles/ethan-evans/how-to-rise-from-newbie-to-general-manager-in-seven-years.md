The hardest part has always been explaining to people what I do.

Living in a city where the first question at every cocktail party is *“So, what do you do?”* was strong motivation to clear this up, so here’s my elevator pitch: I’m General Manager of a leadership development company called Elevate Leadership. The business is led by Robert Glazer, a successful entrepreneur, former CEO, bestselling author, and keynote speaker. We help organizations reach their full potential by building better leaders.

**That’s pretty much right, but it *****hardly***** tells the story.**

**My *****real***** job is part manager, part editor, part consultant, part podcast producer, part graphic designer, part salesperson, part finance manager, part web developer. **When I first started working with Robert, I had no training or qualifications in any of these roles. But Robert has a way of motivating you to teach yourself new skills and not let ‘perfect’ be the enemy of ‘done’.

In seven years working with Robert, we’ve launched seven books, built his Elevate Podcast to 1.5 million downloads per year, and scaled his speaking career to the point that he delivered over 40 speeches in 2025. 

This work has been occasionally challenging, deeply rewarding, and a daily masterclass with a truly accomplished business leader.

Today’s business climate is defined by upheaval and anxiety. Automation is coming for many professional roles, but no one can know which ones, and when. **I’ve come to believe that one enduring skillset will be the ability to build trusting partnerships with successful leaders and creators—people who need someone to manage the business of being a sought-after expert.**

**Given that, I’d encourage any professional to do what I did by accident: *****partner with someone successful who you believe in and who believes in you.*** Learn as much as you can in the process. Trust that those lessons, and that relationship, will steady you in choppy waters.

The Right Person

When I first met Robert in 2015, I was an entry-level employee at a member association called Entrepreneurs’ Organization (EO), and he was a face in a box on a 6pm Zoom call. My title was Leadership Coordinator, and I helped organize leadership training programs for entrepreneurs. My first project was EO’s flagship leadership training program, “Global Leadership Academy”, and Robert was on the member committee overseeing the event. 

When we met on that first Zoom call, he was grilling dinner, which was his justification for taking time away from his family to join a meeting on a Friday evening. He struck me as funny, insightful, and, per the grilling, someone who had their priorities in order.

I didn’t imagine I’d work for Robert, but from that call onward, I found myself seeking his approval. When I learned he was curious about a data analysis of EO’s global leadership applicant pool, I worked until almost midnight crafting a report with that data, just for him. 

I read his first book, a deep dive on affiliate marketing called *Performance Partnerships*, just so that I could be prepared to talk about it with him if the opportunity arose. 

Years later, we’ve never discussed it. 

Life’s funny that way.

Building a professional partnership is like forging any type of relationship. It requires understanding what the other person cares about and cultivating an appreciation of those same things. For the partnership to last, this shared interest has to be sincere, not forced nor strategic. I would never have lasted with Robert if I didn’t care as much about leadership development as he does.

After a few years of working together at EO, I joined Robert’s company, a marketing agency called Acceleration Partners (AP), in a niche role titled Leadership Marketing Specialist. This past spring, after six years working closely with AP’s executive team, I left the company to join Robert full-time at Elevate Leadership, which he started after exiting AP. 

I did this because I love the work, but also because I believe in Robert. 

There are a few big reasons why.

**We Have Trust: **Working closely with Robert, I have seen how he behaves when no one is watching and how he treats people. He treats people with respect and integrity, even when he doesn’t have to and, even when people don’t show him the same. He does not jeopardize his relationships to get a step ahead. I believe he has my back. This is table stakes for any partnership, but it shouldn’t be ignored.

**We’re Aligned on Values:** Any successful personal or professional relationship must be built on shared core values. Robert and I are both highly curious; we’re obsessed with learning and trying new things. We both like figuring things out ourselves. We both value discipline and good habits. We both believe the long view must always come first, even when it’s inconvenient. In some ways we’re polar opposites, but being aligned on these principles builds a foundation of trust and allow us to have productive conflict.

**He Makes Me Want To Get Better: **Robert unlocked my intrinsic drive to push myself to improve, learn constantly, and go the extra mile. This drive was not present in my high school or college career. I needed a mentor who believed in my potential, but also pushed me hard to reach that potential. Robert tells me when I do something well, but more importantly, he tells me when I need to do something better in a respectful but undeniable manner. 

Once you find someone who unlocks that intrinsic drive for you, stick with them as long as possible. 

There are a few ways to do this.

**First, seek out people who are more accomplished and senior than you in your field, especially at your company.** At EO, I always raised my hand to support additional projects led by EO members I was interested in getting to know. I would also ask my direct manager or department leader if I could get involved in projects involving senior leadership. Leaders generally love it when you show enthusiasm for what they are invested in, so doing this is a way to get attention from the right people.

**Second, be curious when meeting senior people.** As a conversation starter, I recommend asking leaders what they are working on that they’re most excited about. Ask them what books they like to read, what podcasts they listen to, or what they’re most passionate about outside of work. Most of us like to talk about ourselves at least a little bit, and showing genuine curiosity conveys the respect necessary to build a professional partnership.

**Finally, seek out mentorship opportunities, whether through a professional learning organization in your field, at conferences, or even at your own organization.** I was lucky that EO gave me access to many potential mentors, but there are many opportunities available to meet senior mentors in person or digitally. When you do this, to paraphrase John Kennedy: ask not what they can do for you, ask what you can do for them. Showing a genuine willingness to give first earns trust and makes people more likely to mentor or partner with you down the line.

Finding the right mentor or professional partner isn’t easy, but it’s simple. 

Find someone who has your back, cares genuinely about what matters most to you, and constantly inspires you to get better. 

I’m far from reaching my full potential, but if I do, it will be because of Robert.

Beyond personal actualization, working with Robert has delivered a daily dose of lessons on leadership and professional performance.

These are the most essential leadership factors that have formed the backbone of our partnership and grown our business; they can be applied to teams and organizations of *any* industry and size.

1) Create and Act With Alignment

If Robert has a superpower, it’s alignment. He’s figured out exactly what he wants and is laser-focused on doing those things first. He also purposefully deprioritizes everything else. I’ve known this about Robert for years, but it wasn’t until recently that I realized how he does it.

It was about 7am and we were at a conference in Nashville. I was stepping out to get breakfast and ran into Robert as he pored over a few pieces of paper on the hotel patio.

The pages contained Robert’s short and long-term priorities. He had a few annual goals, each one tied to a five-year goal. Each of those annual goals laddered down to a quarterly goal. And he was carefully noting small things he would do that day to make progress toward those quarterly goals, taking daily steps toward his annual and five-year goals in the process.

**I learned that Robert does this ritual every single day. **He reviews his goals and brainstorms things to move him toward those objectives. We have our morning huddle where we go over those tasks and decide who will do what. Then, we do those things before noon. No matter what happens the rest of the day, Robert has made progress toward his highest priorities.

Again, it’s not easy, but it’s simple. 

To get into alignment, start with these steps:

Set aside an hour or two to focus on identifying your goals, both at work and in life. For example, you might set a goal to get promoted in the next year.

Break those large goals into smaller ones that you can accomplish, or make progress toward, in a single day. For example, if you’re seeking a promotion, craft a list of skills you need to learn, certifications you need to earn, habits you need to implement, and relationships you need to strengthen to earn that elevation. Put that list somewhere you will see it every day, such as in a journal you can review with breakfast, or in the notes app on your phone.

Look at that list of smaller goals every morning. Ask yourself: what is one thing I can do today–before noon, if possible–to either accomplish or make progress toward that small goal? It may be as small as signing up for an online course, or reading a chapter of a management book. Small, consistent effort toward the right objectives compounds over time.

If you have the awareness to identify what’s most important and the discipline to review those things and execute them, you’ll succeed more often than not.

2) Leverage Matters Most

Whether you’re operating a small company like I am or supporting a senior person at a larger organization, **the most valuable thing you can do for that leader is create leverage for them. **

Do what it takes to make it easy for them to focus on things that they are excellent at doing and are most important to the team or organization.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!16rd!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F43855429-ae9b-419e-8020-3e64b5a0db64_1341x955.png)

Robert has a few core priorities of our business that he is far better at than I am. 

He is excellent at brainstorming strategy and identifying big bets. He easily finds the right people to partner with and builds those relationships. He is exceptional at creating new content ideas: a seemingly endless tide of articles, posts, books, and speeches.

I’ve designed my role around enabling him to do those things. Much of that involves me doing the things I excel at that complement his skills.

**For example, Robert is a great creator and I am an excellent polisher.** He can give me an article draft or a presentation outline that is messy and vague; he doesn’t need to spend time refining because he knows I can turn it into a finished product that fits his vision.

Similarly, he is great at cultivating relationships with potential clients and partners. Fortunately, I excel at working with those people on tactical steps for our collaboration, laying out plans, following up with next steps, and ultimately closing deals.

Of course, part of creating leverage is taking on the grunt work that is necessary, but not worth Robert’s time. Proofreading posts. Setting up invoices. Getting three podcast episodes per week published properly, to our sponsors’ satisfaction. I don’t like doing these things, but I recognize my job is to make sure they don’t require an ounce of Robert’s energy. He needs as much time as possible to generate the ideas that will set the course for our business tomorrow while I keep things running today.

As our revenue has grown, I have tried to learn from Robert by finding my own points of leverage. 

There are a few things you can do to maximize your own leverage:

List of the tasks and responsibilities that are most essential to success in your role or drive vital outcomes for your organization. It’s good to start with three to five ideas so that you narrow your focus to the most vital things. These are **your essentials**.

Once you’ve listed those essentials, circle the ones you are either excellent at now, or believe you can become great at with time and effort. These things, representing what you are uniquely positioned to do well that are vital to individual and organizational success, are your **points of leverage.**

List the other things that you do on a regular basis that you did not include in the essentials. For each one, determine if you can automate it, if you can delegate it, or if it needs to be done at all. Moving these low-priority tasks off your desk creates time to double down on your points of leverage.

This year, I’ve carved out time to do this myself, looking where we can rely on contractors or automation, so that I can focus on my own top points of leverage.

That’s an ongoing battle, but Robert has taught me the importance of letting go.

3) Constantly Iterate

Robert’s top core value is “Find A Better Way and Share It”. 

He is a constant tinkerer and a maven for new ideas. A recurring mantra is “I’ll try anything once.”

I’ll confess this sometimes annoys me. 

I am much more of a skeptic and can be reluctant to mess with something that’s working well. But I’ve come to see the benefit of Robert’s approach.
