AI is automating skills and disrupting jobs. 

To survive this professional extinction event, it is critical to do two seemingly contradictory things: *master AI tools and invest in soft skills.* 

**We call this being AI Plus (AI+).  **

The winners of the AI age will use AI well and have deep mastery over other expressly human skills.

In this article, we will frame the problem and then quickly outline specific steps you can take to protect and enhance your career amid these disruptions. 

To summarize, it is critical to go beyond simply using AI well to also investing in your higher-level social and decision-making skills. 

This is the idea behind being “AI+” (more than AI alone).

Where the Trend is Going

Most people recognize the power of AI tools, even if they fear their impact.

At this time, AI tools have already taken over most computer programming and are rapidly expanding into accounting, law, administration, and other fields that are based on applying fixed sets of rules to large bodies of data. 

See the below image from Anthropic’s “Labor market impacts of AI” report on LLM occupation capability (note: chart does not include robotics / hardware which means the blue area is broader).

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PN0q!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc9ed6f29-50e6-4758-aa55-6a966bde193f_837x835.png)

In this environment, there are three trends that seem certain to continue:

AI tools are likely to keep getting better at these systematic skills.

The set of skills that the tools are good at will expand with training and use.

The tools will get easier to use, allowing people without coding, accounting, or legal skills to use them competently.

If we take those things as true, it is a mistake to think about AI in terms of what it can do today. To protect and grow your career, you need to anticipate what is coming.

A great example of the speed of this progression is “vibe coding.” The term “vibe coding” was first used on February 3, 2025 (just over a year ago) to describe the idea of producing software without ever looking at the code. Since then, more stories are emerging of non-coders creating meaningful, valuable software.

I’ve tested this idea myself. 

I have not written software since leaving graduate school in 1994.  

32 years later, I was able to build simple web utilities using OpenAI’s Codex tool in just a few hours. I built these applications without writing a line of code, in a language I do not know (JavaScript) on top of a stack I never used (React).

Think about that: I personally produced my first software in over 30 years in just a few hours. 

If I can do that, anyone can.

Very soon, producing software will be no harder than using your iPhone. Once that happens, calling yourself a computer programmer will be as irrelevant as listing Microsoft Word as a skill on your resume. It will be so table-stakes that it’s not worth mentioning.

The Race Off the Cliff

Today, many people are seeking to differentiate themselves by being the best with each new AI tools as it emerges. With AI allowing software developers to produce code at 5x or 10x their historic rate, mastering these tools can set you apart in the near-term. The same is true in other fields.

Historically, mastering difficult tools has been an effective way to set yourself apart for the long term. Master crafters, whether cabinet makers or coders, have been able to command premium rates.

The race today is **different**.

AI is the first tool that learns and improves from being used. The more systems it builds, the better it gets at building. And, these tools get more accessible and easy to use every day. 

As we use the tools, we are both racing other users and the learning curve of the tools themselves.

You are essentially training your replacement by using these tools, so “mastering them” is a very short-sighted strategy. 

You are accelerating your own obsolescence; racing towards the edge of the cliff.

The AI+ Approach

In the book *[Open to Work](https://www.amazon.com/Open-Work-How-Get-Ahead/dp/0063486466)*, authors Ryan Roslansky and Aneesh Raman propose a three-bucket method for systematically developing your skills.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!j9YP!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb06cc4e7-6fd5-44cf-981e-bf0391e7de66_970x454.png)

Bucket 1 — Tasks that AI can Automate

These tasks follow a general pattern of “if this, then that.” 

They are systematic and governed by rules, even if those rules are complex, like the Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) or the syntax and libraries of a mature coding language.

**Example tasks:**

Producing routine reports

Summarizing meetings and emails

Data entry

Scheduling

Answering FAQs

Simple and moderate coding

Here, their advice matches what many are doing — learn to use AI tools well to automate this work quickly and completely. 

It is going to be automated, so being an expert in the tools is a necessary job skill, just as learning to use a PC was in the 80s or learning to use Google was in the 90s.

Bucket 2 — Tasks that AI can Assist with Today

The second bucket contains tasks that AI can assist with today, but where humans make the final adjustments. 

These are often the tasks people complain about as “AI Slop” today. 

“AI Slop” refers to low-quality work produced largely or completely with AI, and that many consumers recognize as AI output. Slop results from using AI for a task it cannot fully handle, then shipping that output without human intervention.

An obvious example today is writing. 

AI can draft reports and email responses, but humans must still add context from outside the system and decide what parts of the report are most relevant. Most people can detect when a response is pure AI, with no human editing.

The pattern for bucket 2 tasks is that there is a clear part where AI can assist with gathering and preparing the raw materials, but there is also a second step that is currently beyond what AI can do.

**Three examples:**

Using AI to analyze data and find patterns, but then determining which of those patterns actually matter to your specific business yourself.

Having AI draft content that is technically correct, but then using your personal experience to make that content emotionally resonant.

Using AI to summarize the background for customer interactions, but then using your human intuition to read between the lines for what is not being said.

In bucket 2, you want to get good at quickly using AI tools to do the first part of the task, leaving you with more time to do a great job on the human part of the job.

It is important to notice what skills the human is applying in these examples: **Judgment, contextualization, emotional awareness, and human communication skills.** 

These skills are usually called “soft skills” in contrast to things like mathematics or law, which are “hard” skills.

The simple truth is that “hard” skills have clear patterns and rules, which makes them much easier for AI to automate.  

To advance our careers, that means further developing and emphasizing our “soft” skills, which are much more difficult for AI to automate. 

For those in “hard skill” professions, like software engineering, we now use our hard skills in programming not to do the task, but to understand what can be done, explain what AI has done, and contextualize it for others. 

The value of our hard skills is not lost, but it moves from an active value to a supervisory value.

Over time, as AI improves, it will get better at some skills in bucket 2.  It is unclear exactly how fast this will happen, but my guess is that many bucket 2 skills will slide at least partway into bucket 1 over the next three years. 

**That gives you three years to develop your soft skills to protect and grow your career. **

Don’t wait until it is too late. 

Start now.

Bucket 3 — Skills That Are Uniquely Human

As you will see, almost all of these involve live interactions between people, and they remain human because AI is not a part of this process.

**Three examples of valuable business skills where AI is uninvolved today:**

Calming a nervous client by sensing their unspoken fears.

Mediating between colleagues whose conflict is upending the team.

Knowing when to break from standard procedure because the situation is different.

**Common elements of bucket 3 skills:**

Reading unspoken dynamics

Building trust

Navigating ambiguity

Creating something genuinely new

Personal or Executive Presence

Likability

Displaying authentic empathy

These are classic soft skills. 

The good news about such skills is that they can be learned, practiced, and improved.

In fact, we can use the time that is freed up after AI has automated our bucket 1 tasks for developing our skills here.

**This, again, is the meaning of AI+. **

We use AI to free our time from repetitive tasks with clear rules. 

This requires that we be good at using AI tools. 

But we do not stop there. 

We add the “plus” to actively invest in and develop our human skills.

Here are your key soft skills, how you can improve them, and why they will get you a job.

1. Networking

Networking is a soft skill. You do it by building relationships and trust with other people. **It is essential because we know that 80% of new leadership jobs are never posted**, so you can only learn about them through your network. Furthermore, 80% of jobs, even those that are posted publicly, go to people with a personal connection to the role.

In an AI world where every job posting attracts 10,000+ applications, your network and relationships will be among your most powerful and defensible moats.

I’ve built [a whole class](https://ethan-evans.mykajabi.com/Leadership-Networking) on how anyone, including introverts, can build their network in an enjoyable way, without ever attending a cocktail party or networking event.

A second reason that networking is essential is that your **network is also the best way for a hiring manager to validate the claims you make on your resume**. When someone they know and trust tells them that you are the right person for the job, that will be stronger than any claim you make in your application or on your resume.

However, it is also important for you to showcase your soft skills on your resume. The key to doing this effectively is to **“show, not tell.”**  It is important to support your list of soft skills with examples and accomplishments that demonstrate your mastery of those abilities.

For presence or communication skills, add your talks, publications, or documents as links to your resume. Let readers see for themselves what you can do instead of just having to take your word for it. 

For skills like negotiation or coordination, document how many teams and people were a part of a project or accomplishment.

**Three other ways your network can help you:**

Increases your luck surface area. The more people you know and the more value you add, the more opportunities you will encounter.

Access to capital is an underrated network benefit (e.g. ability to raise money). Having high social capital will give you easier access to financial capital. 

The “side hustle” era is evolving into a true “Multi-SKU” career where knowledge workers build a broader portfolio of skills (using AI) to diversify income and not be reliant on companies investing to replace them with AI. This will be the creator economy 2.0, and it helps to have a vast network in which to offer these services. 

We’ve seen [Level Up Community members](https://levelupwithethanevans.substack.com/about) build real relationships by hosting and attending events like the AI Paper Reading Club, AI Engineering Club, virtual accountability meetups, networking events (virtual and in-person), peer mentoring roundtables, and more.

2. Decision-Making

This is one of the biggest bucket 3 skills because people are not ready to fully trust AI with the final decisions about their time and money. 

For this reason, making good decisions quickly and communicating them convincingly is a key skill.

There is a process to making good decisions, and AI can support you in the research and planning parts of that process. However, most decisions involve unknowns, ambiguity, and taking risks. Even if AI makes a clear recommendation, it will ultimately be a human who must accept that recommendation, sell it to others, and follow up with execution.

**Things to keep in mind about decision-making:**

Committing to difficult decisions is emotionally challenging. Humans need to be led through tough decisions and will not blindly trust an AI recommendation when it feels scary or unclear.

A thorough monitoring and adjustment plan is part of a good decision. AI cannot see and monitor all aspects of life, so humans must ultimately assess circumstances and results in the real world.

When something goes wrong with a plan or there is a mistake in execution, humans are going to want to see a leader take responsibility and be in charge of the situation. No one wants to hear “the AI will take care of it” when there is a problem. Taking clear responsibility in a crisis is a human skill that can build trust.

4. Executive Presence

Executive Presence (EP) may be the ultimate soft skill because people struggle to even define it. Other people decide how much EP you have based on their own expectations and criteria.  

That said, EP can be broken down into discrete components that can be learned and tuned. The good news is, only about 30% of your presence is based on your speaking skills, which means both introverts and those who speak English as a second language can improve dramatically without focusing on public speaking.

**Three examples:**

The two biggest factors in your perceived EP are your confidence and decisiveness. AI can give a confidence interval to an answer, but it cannot inspire confidence in others.

You cannot carry an AI-based decision into a room with CEOs, the press, or investors and just hand them a sheet of paper. They want to hear from you, not your toolset.

In meetings and with people, you inspire confidence by answering questions quickly and authoritatively. **“Let me ask my AI” is NOT an option.**

5. Emotional Intelligence (EQ)

Contrary to popular belief, EQ is not about being touchy-feely. 

It’s knowing how to manage your emotional state and your relationships to accomplish your goals. It’s having your emotions work *for* you rather than *against* you.

Since emotions affect your decision-making, performance and creativity, focusing ability, relationships, and physical and mental health (and you experience them over 90% of the work day), EQ has a big impact on your ability to perform at a high level and sustain that performance.

**Psychologist Daniel Goleman breaks down EQ into four domains:**

Self-awareness: understanding your emotional state and how you’re coming across to others.

Self-management: regulating your emotions productively to achieve your goals.

Social awareness: understanding what other people are thinking and feeling, reading their context, and picking up on what’s not being said.

Relationship management: navigating social interactions effectively, which includes building trust, influencing, and resolving conflict.

**Data points worth paying attention to:**

McKinsey** **projects that by 2030, up to a third of work activities will be automated. The hardest to automate? Tasks requiring social and emotional skills.

The World Economic Forum states that six of the ten most critical skills for future   success are social-emotional in nature: resilience and adaptability, leadership and social influence, motivation and self-awareness, empathy and active listening, creative thinking, and curiosity and lifelong learning.

LinkedIn identifies conflict mitigation and adaptability as two of the top three fastest-growing skills needed for professional success.

Notice that ***adaptability*** is mentioned twice, and for good reason. 

Nobody really knows what the future will look like, so being highly flexible—cognitively, emotionally, and socially—will be essential to thrive and succeed in our rapidly changing world.

EQ is not just another skill in the stack. 

It’s the meta-skill that undergirds the other human skills. 

You can learn cognitive techniques for communication, creativity, reading the room, or influence, but without EQ, those skills have limited efficacy. 

EQ is what unlocks timing, judgment, empathy, and trust—qualities that elevate human skills from adequate to exceptional.

Your General Recipe for Any Soft Skill

The pattern here is straightforward even if it is not easy.  

Do the work to identify the parts of your job that rely on things AI struggles to automate. This often includes situations where you are interacting with other people about the work rather than sitting at your desk cranking out an artifact. Then, determine what skills help you to be successful at those parts of your job, and where you could improve.

EQ and soft skills can absolutely be learned. Once you identify what you need to learn, approach that just like you approach the challenge of keeping up with rapidly evolving AI tools. 

You know how to learn; focus some of your time on learning these AI-resistant skills.

**Your future job security depends on you being AI+. **

This means being good with the tools so you are efficient at producing a lot of work, but also bringing a lot of value beyond the tools so that the tools alone cannot replace you, even as they improve.

Your Job Right Now — Be Proactive and Become AI+ Today

To summarize:

Divide your key skills and workplace activities into the three buckets.

Start automating things in bucket 1.

Use the time you save to invest in the human skills of bucket 2 and bucket 3.

Don’t rely on good intentions. Take a clear action, such as signing up for a course, that commits you to putting time and effort into the skills you need for your future.

The change is happening right now. 

You can be ahead or you can fall behind.
