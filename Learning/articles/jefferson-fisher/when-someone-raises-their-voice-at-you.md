# When Someone Raises Their Voice at You

**Source:** https://jeffersonfisher.substack.com/p/when-someone-raises-their-voice-at
**Ingested:** 2026-04-02
**Tags:** communication, soft-skills

---

I want to tell you about a moment that changed the way I handle conflict.

Early in my career, I was in a heated deposition. The opposing attorney older, louder, clearly used to getting his way leaned across the table and raised his voice at me. Not just frustrated. Full volume. Right in my face.

I remember feeling my heart rate spike. My jaw tighten. Every instinct in my body told me to match his energy to push back, to prove I wasn't someone who could be talked to that way.

I didn't.

I waited. I let the silence sit. And then I spoke quietly, slowly, calmly.

The shift in the room was immediate. His volume had made him look out of control. My quiet made me look unshakeable.

That moment taught me something I carry with me to this day: **the person who stays calm always holds the most power in the room.**

Why People Raise Their Voice

Before we talk about what to do, it helps to understand what's actually happening when someone raises their voice at you.

Yelling is almost never about you.

It's about them. It's what happens when someone runs out of better tools. When the feelings are too big and the words feel too small. When they're overwhelmed, scared, embarrassed, or backed into a corner they don't know how to get out of.

That doesn't make it okay. It just makes it understandable.

And when you understand what's driving the behavior, you stop taking it so personally — and you start responding instead of reacting.

What Most People Do (And Why It Makes Things Worse)

When someone raises their voice, most people do one of two things:

They **match the energy** — raise their voice back, get defensive, say something sharp. The argument escalates. Things get said that can't be unsaid. Nobody wins.

Or they **shut down** — go quiet, shrink, say whatever it takes to make it stop. The conflict ends on the surface, but the resentment builds underneath.

Neither of those responses actually resolves anything. They just move the problem around.

There's a third option. And it's the one most people never think to use.

The Calm Response That Changes Everything

Here's what I want you to try the next time someone raises their voice at you.

**Lower yours.**

Not because you're surrendering. Not because you're weak. But because you understand something they don't in that moment — that calm is contagious, and so is chaos. You get to choose which one spreads.

When you lower your voice, one of two things happens: they either come down to meet you, or they realize how loud they sound and feel it. Either way, you've shifted the dynamic without saying a single confrontational word.

**Then pause before you speak.**

A two or three second pause after someone yells at you is one of the most powerful things you can do. It signals that you are not rattled. That you are in control of yourself. That what they just said is not going to pull you off your foundation.

**Then say this:**

"*I'm happy to continue this conversation when the volume comes down*."

That's it. Simple. Direct. No blame, no shame, no escalation. Just a clear, calm boundary that tells them exactly what you need without turning it into a fight.

You Are Allowed to Hold Your Ground

I want to say something that I think a lot of people need to hear.

You do not have to absorb someone else's anger just to keep the peace.

Staying calm is not the same as staying silent. It's not the same as accepting bad treatment. It's not weakness dressed up as patience.

Staying calm is a choice  a deliberate, courageous one  to refuse to let someone else's emotional chaos become yours.

You are allowed to say "*I won't be spoken to that way*." You are allowed to pause the conversation. You are allowed to leave the room and come back when things have settled.

What you are not required to do is stand there and take it.

The Bigger Picture

Here's what I've seen over and over again  in courtrooms, in coaching conversations, in the messages you send me every day:

The people who communicate best under pressure are not the ones who never feel rattled. They are the ones who have decided ahead of time how they are going to show up, no matter what comes at them.

You can be that person.

It starts with one conversation. One moment where you choose calm over chaos. One time you lower your voice instead of raising it, pause instead of reacting, hold your ground without losing your composure.

That one moment builds something. And then the next one does too.

Before long, people around you start to notice. They stop pushing as hard. They start listening differently. Because somewhere along the way, you became the person in the room that nobody can shake.

That's the goal. Not to be louder. To be steadier.

You've already got what it takes. You just have to decide to use it.
