# Goals (Next 6–12 Months)

## North Star
- **[Goal]** Grow into a Director-caliber leader by building things that genuinely matter at scale — through others, through craft, and through a stable inner foundation that doesn't depend on external validation to function.
- **[Why it matters]** The ambition is real: more scope, more impact, more organizational leverage. But the path there has to be built on internal clarity, not external approval. The Director title is a byproduct of becoming the right kind of leader — not the proof of it.
- **[Success definition]**
  - You pursue the Director trajectory because you want to build at scale — not because you need it to feel successful.
  - Leadership routes ambiguous / high-stakes problems to you because you're the right person, not because you lobbied for it.
  - Your org executes without you being in the critical path for everything.
  - You recover quickly when knocked off balance — by a reorg, a Slack thread, or an ambiguous comment in a meeting — and return to your base without prolonged rumination.

## Top Outcomes (ranked)

### 0) Build and maintain the inner foundation: mental resilience and emotional stability
- **[Rationale]** Everything else on this list depends on this. The Tai Chi base. When the world throws something at you — a reorg, a skip-level's warm comment to a peer, an ambiguous rating — how quickly do you return to center? This is not a soft goal. It is the foundation that makes the outer game possible.
- **[What "done" looks like]**
  - Rumination episodes are shorter and less frequent. You notice the spiral starting and interrupt it faster.
  - You stop reading tea leaves from managers' tones, VP reactions, or thumbs-up counts on Slack posts.
  - Your sense of whether you're doing good work comes primarily from your own internal standard — not from others' signals.
  - You can hold the Director ambition without needing it to validate your worth.
- **[Leading signals]**
  - Recovery time from a triggering event shortens measurably (hours, not days).
  - You leave high-stakes meetings asking "did I say what was true and useful?" before "how did I land?"
  - The internal scoreboard feels more real than the external one.
- **[Internal scoreboard — what's actually mine]**
  - Is Pinvestigator measurably better this week than last?
  - Is my team energized and growing?
  - Am I learning something real about agentic systems?
  - Am I recovering faster when knocked off balance?
  - At the end of the week: do *I* think I did good work?
- **[Practice]**
  - Continue working with coach. Visualize the Tai Chi base before high-stakes interactions.
  - Name the pattern out loud when it fires: "I'm reading tea leaves again."
  - Ask one factual question when the spiral starts: "What do I actually know right now?"
- **[Risks]**
  - High-stakes events (reorgs, ratings, peer comparisons) will still trigger the pattern.
  - Insight without practice doesn't change anything.
- **[Mitigations]**
  - Treat inner work as a non-negotiable weekly practice, not a crisis response.
  - Keep the internal scoreboard written down and visible.
- **[Confidence]** Medium-High (awareness is high; consistency of practice is the remaining variable).

### A) Develop genuine judgment as an ML engineering leader in the age of AI

- **[What this means]**
  The most durable and valuable thing an ML engineering manager can develop right now is not a technical skill set — it's judgment. Specifically: the ability to look at a complex sociotechnical system (humans, models, data pipelines, organizational incentives, product surfaces) and make good calls about where to invest, what to trust, and which bets will compound versus dead-end.

  This kind of judgment can't be faked and can't be acquired passively. It comes from building real things, watching them fail, and understanding why. It comes from having an honest eval practice — knowing what your systems actually do, not what you hope they do. It comes from years of watching bets play out and developing the pattern recognition that separates good intuition from confident noise.

- **[What "done" looks like]**
  - You can walk into a technical review of an agentic system, a retrieval architecture, or a model platform decision and ask the questions that matter — about failure modes, eval integrity, trust boundaries, and compounding value — not just execution status.
  - Your intuition about which bets to make is trusted by people who are more technically deep than you. Not because you outcode them, but because your judgment has been right more often than not.
  - You have a genuine eval practice: you know the difference between a system that works and one that merely appears to work. You treat evals as first-class, not as a checkbox.
  - When Rajat or Dylan asks "should we do X?" you have a considered, evidence-grounded view — not a political one.

- **[How to build it]**
  - Pinvestigator is the training ground. Every eval you write, every failure mode you discover, every architectural decision you make and revisit is building this judgment in a real domain.
  - UPP is the larger canvas. The pretraining/fine-tuning paradigm, cross-surface representation learning, and the trust questions around shared base models — these are exactly the kinds of problems that develop the judgment that matters at scale.
  - Make it a practice to be wrong in public occasionally, on small things, and to debrief honestly. Judgment improves fastest when failure is treated as data.

- **[Leading indicators]**
  - Senior engineers on your team or adjacent teams seek your input on technical direction — not just your approval.
  - You catch architectural risks or eval gaps that others missed, consistently.
  - Your intuitions about which projects will compound and which won't start landing more often than not.

- **[Confidence]** Medium-High (the inputs are in place; it's about consistency and honest self-assessment over 18 months).

### B) Build a team that can genuinely work with AI — not just use it

- **[What this means]**
  The world your team is moving into is one where AI handles an increasing share of execution: code generation, experiment analysis, debugging, documentation, data pipeline work. The engineering managers who will be most valuable are not the ones who preserve the old model of what an ML engineer does. They're the ones who help their teams figure out what excellent engineering looks like when AI is a first-class collaborator.

  This is a reskilling challenge, but it's subtle. It's not about teaching people to use Cursor or Claude. It's about helping engineers develop new forms of judgment: when to trust AI outputs and when not to, how to evaluate AI-generated work, how to design systems where human and AI contributions are legible and auditable, and how to stay genuinely sharp in a world where the easy parts of the job are increasingly automated.

- **[What "done" looks like]**
  - Your team has a genuine working culture around AI-assisted engineering — not as a novelty but as a normal part of how work gets done.
  - Your engineers are more productive and more capable because of AI, not more dependent and less able to reason independently.
  - At least 2–3 engineers on your team have developed real fluency in agentic workflows, eval design, or AI-assisted ML development that didn't exist before.
  - You have a clear point of view on what "senior ML engineer" means in 2027, and you're actively developing people toward that profile rather than the 2022 version.
  - Pinvestigator and similar internal tools are being used and extended by the team — not just built by you.

- **[How to build it]**
  - Make your own AI craft work (Pinvestigator, agentic workflows) visible to the team. Not as a performance but as an invitation. Show the work, share the failures, let people see what the learning process looks like.
  - Create low-stakes opportunities for engineers to experiment with AI-assisted workflows in their existing work. Don't mandate it — create pull.
  - In career conversations, start introducing "AI fluency" as a dimension of growth, not as a separate track but as part of what excellent engineering looks like going forward.
  - Use the guild model: a small group (2–6 people) who are genuinely curious and bought in, building together, creating artifacts that others can learn from.

- **[Leading indicators]**
  - Engineers on your team are proactively exploring AI-assisted approaches without being directed to.
  - You're having career conversations where AI fluency comes up naturally, not just as a performance management checkbox.
  - The gap between your team's velocity and peer teams without this culture starts to become visible.

- **[Risks]**

  - The tooling evolving faster than you can build a stable practice around it.
  - Focusing on the tools (Cursor, agents) rather than the judgment (when to trust, how to eval).
- **[Mitigations]**
  - Lead with use cases, not tools. Show what becomes possible, not what people should learn.
  - Keep the eval practice central — that's the stable skill even as tools change.

- **[Confidence]** Medium (the culture piece is harder than the technical piece; requires patience and modeling, not mandates).
