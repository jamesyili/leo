# Decisive by Chip and Dan Heath — Book Notes

## Core Insight

Pros and cons lists are deeply flawed. Better decisions come from a structured process (WRAP) that counteracts the four villains of decision making.

## Four Villains of Decision Making

### 1. Narrow Framing
- Ask "can I have X AND Y?" — reframe binary terms
- Examples:
  - "Which contractor?" → hire a bunch for phase 1, see actual benefit of working with good ones
  - "Should I buy a new car?" → what's the best way to spend money to make my family better off?
- This limits your choices

### 2. Confirmation Bias
- We go looking for data but unconsciously cook the books
- We pretend we want truth, but we're seeking reassurance
- Smokers more likely to read "smoking doesn't cause cancer" articles
- This leads to biased information informing your choice

### 3. Short-Term Emotional Bias
- Ask: What would our successors do? How would the numbers look on a spreadsheet?
- At these moments, what we need most is perspective
- This makes it difficult to make the right choice even when you know it

### 4. Overconfidence
- We think more than we should about how the future will unfold
- We don't hedge as much as we probably should

## WRAP Framework

### W — Widen Your Options
- **Avoid narrow framing.** What is your actual goal? Where do you want to be in 3/5 years?
- **Opportunity cost:** What is the next best option?
- **Vanishing Options Test:** If your current options disappeared, what would you do?
- **"And" not "or."** Toggle promotion and prevention mindsets. Multi-tracking:
  - Faster decisions
  - Less ego involved
  - Less political
- **Find people who've already solved your problem.** Look externally for options.
- **Look for bright spots internally.** Most positive points in your own data distribution (e.g., lunch time gym visits). Native to your situation, best practices as inspiration.
- **Reactive search → proactive guidelines.** Best practices. Bright spots. Brute force list of questions to spark new insights, discover new options.
- **Analogies for breakthroughs.** Local analogies, regional analogies. Extract the crucial feature of the abstract problem. Think about the problem more abstractly — others may have already solved it.

### R — Reality-Test Your Assumptions
- **Consider the opposite.** Is your hubris getting in the way? Is it the situation or is it you? Force yourself to look at the other side. How might the current situation actually be best?
- **Devil's advocate.** Contrarian to surface real criticisms.
- **Mind your bias.** Even with objective pros and cons, you may believe what you're already inclined to.
- **Tripwires for negative things.** Disagree without becoming disagreeable.
- **Probing vs. general questions.** Consider seniority/authority of who you're asking. Try asking in different ways.
- **Keep an objective, comprehensive list.** Assume positive intent. Ask nonjudgmental questions.
- **Test assumptions explicitly.** You win either way. Have a process.
- **Zoom in and zoom out.**
  - High level and low level. Inside and outside view.
  - Conditional and unconditional probabilities. What are you conditioning on?
  - Base rates to ground the conversation: How long does it typically take? How does your situation compare to the average?
  - Talk to someone with more experience for that situation
  - Experts are bad at predictions, great at providing base rates and descriptive information
- **The closeup.** Be discerning about information quality. What is the speaker's agenda? Can you get closer to the truth? Add texture and temperature. Be the customer. Get into their heads. This adds intuition.
- **Ooch then leap.** How can you do a little bit of the thing without committing fully? Little bets.

### A — Attain Distance Before Deciding
- **Resist emotional manipulation.** Car salesmanship = get them to stop thinking and start feeling. Start with an objective list.
- **10/10/10:** How will you feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?
- **Familiarity breeds preference.** Watch for corporate speak, loss aversion.
- **Reframe the perspective:**
  - What would our successors do?
  - How would you advise a friend?
- **Honor your core principles and values.**
- **Benford's ship exercise.** Two lists.

### P — Prepare to Be Wrong
- **Bookending.** Optimistic and pessimistic scenarios with realistic assumptions. 95% confidence intervals. Think about these separately.
- **Ask in different ways.** Framing changes answers (Asian American president example).
- **Premortem.** How likely and how bad if true? Preparedness.
- **Buffer for ego.** Check for ego distortion.
- **Vaccination effect.** Think about tough challenges and how you'd react. Builds resilience, increases chances of success, better awareness of early signs of failure.
- **Set tripwires.**
  - Counter sunk cost fallacy and momentum bias
  - Forcing functions: Do X if Y metric goes above/below Z
  - Deadlines

## Meta-Principles
- More consistency in decision making. Make better decisions 1% more of the time.
- Tripwires trigger asking questions. Sometimes the hardest part is knowing a decision needs to be made.
- Intuitive decisions: take gut feeling as an important input, then consciously ask how much it matters.
