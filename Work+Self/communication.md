# Communication & Thinking Style (How to work with me) 
  
## Purpose 
A compact, reusable spec for how I prefer to communicate, make decisions, process ambiguity, and where I can get blindsided. This is meant to improve speed, trust, and signal quality—especially in high-stakes contexts.

These are the specific “manager-reviewed” themes that should shape how I communicate and what I improve. 
  
### What’s working (keep doing)

- **Direct, candid clarity:** drives alignment and action; sets a high bar for quality and rigor.

- **Flex communication under ambiguity:** create space for open dialogue on priorities and resourcing; avoid appearing “already decided” when alignment is still forming.

## What good communication looks like to me (practical rules) 
  
### 1) Start with the executive top-line 
- **1–2 sentences:** what happened + so what 
- Then: 
  - **Decision needed** 
  - **Options (A/B/C)** 
  - **Recommendation + rationale** 
  - **Risks + mitigations** 
  - **Ask / next step** 
- If detail exists, attach as appendix or offer: “can go deeper if useful.” 
  
**Calibration add (from manager feedback):** 
- In ambiguous cross-team contexts, pair the exec top-line with **one sentence inviting dialogue**: 
  - “Before we lock this, I want to sanity-check priorities/resourcing with the core group.” 
  - This preserves clarity while signaling openness and avoiding premature closure. 
  
### 2) Make uncertainty explicit (I trust it more) 
- Separate: 
  - **Facts** 
  - **Hypotheses** 
  - **Assumptions** 
  - **What would change our mind** 
- I strongly prefer “here’s what we know / don’t know” over confident storytelling. 
- When stakes are high, label **confidence level** of key claims to reinforce trust and prevent overreach. 
  
### 3) Use constraints as the language of reality 
- I engage fastest when you state constraints up front: 
  - time, headcount, latency/cost, launch risk, dependencies, political constraints. 
- If you ignore constraints, I’ll either (a) dismiss it or (b) take over. 
  
### 4) Bring me decisions, not just problems 
- If you bring a problem, include at least: 
  - the **two best candidate solutions** 
  - the **key tradeoff** 
  - the **fastest test** 
  - what you recommend and why 
  
**Pre-alignment default (for partner/leadership-impacting work):** 
- Also include: 
  - “Who have we pre-aligned with?” 
  - “Who might object and why?” 
  - “What’s the plan to socialize before broadcast?” 
  
### 5) Be crisp about ownership and next steps 
- “Owner, ETA, stop condition” are high-respect signals to me. 
  
**Confidence:** High.

## How to best get through to me (what “unblocks James”)

- **Options:** A / B / C (each 1–2 bullets)

- **Risks:** (top 1–2) + mitigation 
- **What would change our mind:** (one bullet)

  - a **clear measurement plan**

- “Here is what we learned” beats “Here is my opinion.” 
  
### If you want me calm / receptive in a tense moment 
- Lead with: 
  - acknowledgement of constraints + shared goals 
  - “I have two viable paths” 
  - “Let’s pick the smallest irreversible decision first”

## My likely blindsides (tell it like it is) 
  
### 1) Emotional attachment to perceived disapproval (esp. from Dylan / high-status stakeholders) 
- **Pattern:** if I sense coldness or disapproval, I may overthink, seek validation, or tighten up. 
- **Cost:** reduces political range; can make me reactive or overly explanatory. 
- **Counter-move:** separate *facts* from *story*; respond with calm optionality and options. 
  
**Confidence:** High. 
  
### 2) Over-indexing on rigor → under-indexing on narrative simplicity (esp. with leadership) 
- **Pattern:** I can want to explain the full causal chain. 
- **Cost:** execs may lose the thread; trust can drop if I sound uncertain or too detailed. 
- **Counter-move:** lead with the conclusion + decision; detail only on request. 
  
**Confidence:** High. 
  
### 3) Communication rigidity / inconsistency under pressure (tone + pacing) 
- **Pattern:** I default to direct, fast, and “get to the point,” even when the audience needs more context or softness. 
- **Cost:** can create unnecessary friction, especially with product/front-end partners. 
- **Counter-move:** “power softeners” + curious framing; adapt to audience’s incentives. 
  
**Confidence:** High. 
  
### 4) Acting too quickly to collapse ambiguity (without the full picture) 
- **Pattern:** high agency can translate into fast action before all stakeholder context is understood. 
- **Cost:** creates rework, misalignment, or partner resistance; can look like “decision made without dialogue,” especially on priorities/resourcing. 
- **Counter-move:** timebox context-gathering + pre-alignment: “24–48h to collect inputs, then we decide.” Maintain speed via a clear decision date. 
  
**Confidence:** High. 
  
### 5) Becoming the integration bottleneck 
- **Pattern:** because I’m effective, I get pulled into every thread; I also may insert myself to keep quality high. 
- **Cost:** limits Director-level leverage; can signal “org can’t run without me.” 
- **Counter-move:** delegate ownership + keep review gates; promote artifacts over presence. 
  
**Confidence:** Medium-High. 
  
### 6) Political underinvestment (when I wish the system were purely rational) 
- **Pattern:** I prefer good arguments over authority; politics exists anyway. 
- **Cost:** others can shape narratives/credit; misaligned stakeholders can stall outcomes. 
- **Counter-move:** stakeholder mapping, pre-alignment, narrative hygiene, “influence without attachment.” 
  
**Confidence:** Medium-High. 
  
### 7) Under-investing in bonding rituals because velocity feels more “real” 
- **Pattern:** social/bonding time is the first thing sacrificed when workload spikes; events get canceled. 
- **Cost:** weak ties degrade; newer/junior members feel less connected; culture becomes “efficient but thin.” 
- **Counter-move:** treat bonding as operational hygiene: protect lightweight recurring rituals; default to “short + consistent” over “big + rare.” 
  
**Confidence:** High.

## Areas to improve (explicit) 
This section is intentionally “manager-feedback-aligned” and should drive my self-coaching. 
  
1) **Communication consistency + measured delivery (Intent Labeling)** - **What to do:** keep directness, but tune tone/pacing by audience; avoid sounding “already decided” in ambiguous situations. 
- **Practice:** Use **"Intent Labeling"** before disagreeing or pushing for speed. (e.g., *"My intent here is to unblock us for Friday, so I am pushing hard on X. Thoughts?"*) Laying the tone first prevents my directness from being read as aggression, ensuring speed doesn't compromise psychological safety.
- **Failure mode:** crispness becomes bluntness; speed reads as disregard for context. 
  
2) **Pre-alignment before broad communication (esp. cross-team / leadership)**
- **What to do:** raise flags and seek internal alignment first; socialize complex/contentious topics in smaller forums. 
- **Practice:** “small-room pre-wire” before big channels; leverage trusted partners to review major messages. 
- **Failure mode:** broadcast creates avoidable misalignment; partners feel blindsided. 
  
3) **Managing ambiguity: slow down just enough to get the full picture**
- **What to do:** timebox context gathering to prevent premature action. 
- **Practice:** set a 24–48h “input window” + explicit decision checkpoint; define what evidence is required. 
- **Failure mode:** acting quickly drives rework or political friction. 
  
4) **Brevity & executive framing (continue tightening)**
- **What to do:** make messages legible to execs: conclusion first; minimal necessary context; clear ask. 
- **Practice:** 6 bullets max; attach deeper analysis separately. 
- **Failure mode:** over-explaining under stress; loss of executive attention. 
  
5) **Emotional regulation / composure under pressure**
- **What to do:** detach from perceived disapproval; respond with calm optionality. 
- **Practice:** pre-meeting reset; post-meeting debrief: *what did I feel? what story am I telling? what are the facts?* 
- **Failure mode:** rumination or intensity reduces political range. 
  
6) **Scaling through others: delegation + bench strength**
- **What to do:** increase distributed ownership and reduce “James as glue.” 
- **Practice:** delegate ownership with clear gates; invest in structured mentorship and regular career conversations; pair team members with mentors outside the team. 
- **Failure mode:** high bar leads to re-centralization; frontline leaders struggle and I end up cleaning up. 
  
7) **Team bonding and morale as an operational priority**
- **What to do:** protect lightweight, consistent bonding rituals; don’t repeatedly cancel. 
- **Practice:** schedule small recurring events; make them “default on” unless truly critical. 
- **Failure mode:** culture becomes transactional; new/junior members feel less connected.

# Speaking Patterns & Reminders

> Patterns Jarvis should watch for and coach against in James's presentations, meeting contributions, and executive communications. Updated as new patterns are identified.

Last updated: 2026-03-29

---

## Pattern 1: Backstory Scope Creep

**The habit:** Over-narrating system architecture before establishing why the audience should care. Walking through every box in a diagram, every step in a pipeline, before landing the business point.

**Example (ELT March 2026):** Original Slide 3 spent 4 sentences narrating the recommendation funnel (corpus, CGs, lightweight scoring, heavy ranking) before getting to the actual problem (hard-coded constants). The CTO didn't need to know CG1 fetches 500.

**The fix:** Start right before "getting eaten by the bear." Reference the visual ("This is the funnel architecture we share across five surfaces") in one sentence, then go straight to the insight. Let the diagram do the explaining.

**Jarvis prompt:** When reviewing talk tracks, flag any section where 3+ sentences of system description precede the business point. Ask: "Can you compress the setup to one sentence and let the visual carry the rest?"

---

## Pattern 2: Burying the Lead / Delayed BLUF

**The habit:** Building up to the most compelling number or insight instead of leading with it. Saving the punchline for the end of a section when it should be the opening hook.

**Example (ELT March 2026):** The $6.5M cost figure and $938K shipped savings were buried deep in Slide 5 — the last thing in James's section. The audience had to listen to 3+ minutes of problem framing before hearing the stakes.

**The fix:** BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front). State the headline first, then support it. "This is already saving us nearly a million dollars a year while improving engagement" — then explain how.

**Jarvis prompt:** When reviewing any presentation or exec update, identify the single most compelling data point. If it appears after the halfway mark, flag it: "Your strongest number is buried. Can you lead with it?"

---

## Pattern 3: Engaging at the Wrong Altitude

**The habit:** Defaulting to the strategic/coordinator altitude when the audience is buying at a different level. Presenting as "architect of the transition" when the room wants demos and shipped artifacts. Conversely, diving into technical details when leadership wants the business narrative.

**Example (AI Forums / Jeff):** James positioned himself as coordinator of AI demo sessions and "invisible author" of the AI transition. Jeff's recognition email celebrated the people who built and shipped tools (Roberto, Phil, Aravindh). James was invisible.

**The fix:** Read what the audience is buying before choosing your altitude. Jeff in AI forums = "show me the thing." Dylan/Rajat in strategy meetings = "tell me the architecture." Match the altitude to the buyer. When in doubt, have both versions ready.

**Jarvis prompt:** Before any presentation or forum, ask: "What is this audience buying — demos, strategy, or decisions? Are you matching that altitude?"

---

## Pattern 4: Coordinator Trap (Organizing Others' Visibility)

**The habit:** Volunteering to coordinate, curate, and orchestrate other people's presentations — which makes James invisible while making everyone else visible. The Director instinct (orchestrate) applied in a context where the IC instinct (show the work) would be higher leverage.

**Example (AI Forums):** James coordinated the first AI demo session (Matthew, Roberto, Bella). He didn't present. Jeff remembers the presenters, not the coordinator.

**The fix:** When there's a demo slot, take one. Coordinating is fine as a secondary role, but never at the expense of showing your own work. If you organized it, you should also be in it.

**Jarvis prompt:** If James mentions coordinating or organizing a presentation forum, ask: "Are you also presenting? If not, should you be?"

---

## Pattern 5: Over-Explaining Under Pressure (The Rambling Index)

**The habit:** When stakes are high or anxiety is elevated, defaulting to longer answers with more justification. Adding caveats, context, and defensive reasoning that dilute the core point. Anxiety and rambling are correlated (coaching notes: 90-100 with VPs, 10 with family).

**Example (coaching sessions):** Identified by Rodney as a persistent pattern. Rambling increases proportionally with perceived stakes.

**The fix:**
- 3A Pyramid for Q&A: Answer first, Arguments second (2-3 max), Add-ons only if asked.
- 3-2-1 for hard questions: Pause 3 seconds, give 2 points, end with 1 question or close.
- "Authentic talking less" — smile and stop. Don't fill silence with justification.
- Swap "I think" for "I've observed" — more credible, forces concreteness.

**Jarvis prompt:** In mock Q&A, if an answer exceeds 4 sentences, flag it: "That's running long. What's the 2-sentence version?" Also watch for defensive framing ("Well, the reason is..." / "To be fair..." / "I think...").

---

## Pattern 6: Not Validating Negative Frames

**The habit:** When asked about "risk" or "concerns," repeating the negative frame in the answer, which cements it in the audience's mind.

**Example (ELT Q&A prep):** "What's the risk to engagement?" — the trap is opening with "The risk is mitigated because..." which reinforces "risk."

**The fix:** Pivot to positive data without echoing the negative word. "We've actually seen a positive impact on engagement — repins are up 0.51% and latency dropped from 70ms to 15ms." Answer the Question Behind the Question (QBQ): they're really asking "will this break something?" — answer that directly with evidence.

**Jarvis prompt:** In mock Q&A, if James echoes a negative frame from the question ("risk," "concern," "problem"), flag it immediately: "You just validated their negative frame. Pivot to the positive evidence instead."

## Quick Reference: Pre-Presentation Checklist

Before any executive presentation, run through these:

1. **BLUF check:** Is the strongest number/insight in the first 30 seconds?
2. **Altitude check:** What is this audience buying — demos, strategy, or decisions?
3. **Scope creep check:** Any section with 3+ sentences of setup before the point?
4. **Visibility check:** Am I presenting, or just coordinating?
5. **Rambling prep:** Do I have 2-sentence versions of every anticipated answer?
6. **Frame check:** For each likely question, what's the QBQ, and am I pivoting to positive evidence?
